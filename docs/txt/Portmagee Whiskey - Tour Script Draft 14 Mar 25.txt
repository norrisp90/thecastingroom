Section A, providing excellent tours at Portmagee Whiskey

The essential ingredients for a good tour guide are enthusiasm and passion for the product and a genuine liking for interacting with people.  If you have these qualities you are well on the way to being an excellent tour guide. 

Here we provide some tips and hints that can help ensure you deliver an engaging and enjoyable tour. Please read through this document to help you provide a great experience for visitors to Portmagee Whiskey

Characteristics of excellent service
Try to keep as many of these characteristics in mind as possible. You are a representative of Portmagee and will determine to a considerable degree the experience of visiting the attraction.

Different types of communication
There are three types of communication: verbal, vocal and body language. The words you say are only part of the tools of your trade. As important, if not more, is your vocal communication and your body language. Be aware of how you stand, your movements, the pitch of your voice, how fast you speak etc. Some of your guests may not have English or Gaelic as their first language and will welcome a slower delivery and clear enunciation. 

How to make a good first impression:
• Smile
• Welcome guests with an appropriate greeting as soon as possible
• Thank guests for waiting if there is a queue
• Offer help and assistance
• Be pro-active – be prepared to approach guests and start a conversation
• If you are busy, acknowledge guests who are waiting
• Apologise for any service delays
• Look professional and confident
• Be efficient and professional
• Don’t use your mobile in view of guests



Efficient and responsive service
• Introduce yourself at the start of any tour
• Explain how long the tour will take at the start, and where it will finish
• Outline briefly what guests will see and/or do
• Mention subject of health and safety – fire exit, tripping hazards, toilets etc.
• Find out if this is a first or return visit for members of the group and adjust what you say accordingly
• Make sure everyone can hear you
• Pay special attention to any children or elderly visitors in the group
• Give guests opportunities to ask questions – be sure to repeat questions for the benefit of the wider group
• Make your commentary engaging and entertaining
• Speak clearly and confidently – and at an appropriate pace. Do not use jargon.
• Watch for any guests who are struggling to keep up
• Manage your time effectively
• Consider weather conditions; don’t keep guests hanging around in wet or cold if possible
Be aware of the changing seasons and different times of day and how this may affect a tour. What is the position of the sun, for example, are you standing with your back to the light making it difficult for people to look in your direction or see your features?

Types of guests on a tour
Guests on a tour can be divided into three groups. You are likely to have a mix of these types on many tours and the key to success is identifying all three and satisfying each of them. 

Paddlers – These are guests that aren’t concerned with receiving too much information and just want an enjoyable atmosphere. Headline topics, few facts and figures and more back and forth fun engagement is preferred here.

Swimmers – These are guest that have an interest or understanding in general of whiskey, Portmagee or history in general. Try to get your dates correct for them and allow them to read the information boards in their own time to give them further detail. You will likely get from their body language that they are following your lead and interested in hearing the details in so far as you have them available.

Divers – These are guests that may well know more about history, whiskey etc than you do, so do not bluff answers that you aren’t sure off. They may be inclined to ask probing questions in which you should satisfy to the depth of your own knowledge and offer the opportunity to discuss in more detail after the tour is concluded or point to the info boards for further info. Allow engagement but do not let them take over the tour with too much of a story as they will interfere with the running order and potentially impinge on the other guest’s interest as well as going off topic. Remember, you will always know more about our overall plan and the genuineness about our efforts, this is what you can focus on. The correct dates, extra historical events etc are nice to know but you retain the closest connection of the group to the key information that is need to know and is the crux of what the tour is about.



Section B: tour content

Information and delivery techniques can be learned and in this section we provide information that should be included in what you say about Portmagee and highlight ways that you can support the success of the business. 

The core content of the tour should focus on the story of Portmagee but be aware of the wider history of the South West coast of Ireland. Many guests from outside Ireland or from other parts of Ireland may know very little about the area and will be interested in information about the locality 

Make the tour interesting by painting a picture and be expressive in the telling of it. Try to bring to life the people and events that have shaped Portmagee.

Information that is published on the display information boards can be used to construct a tour script. It is reproduced as Appendix A. Appendix B provides script guidance suggesting what to include in a tour and is presented as a table with space for the tour guides notes. A summary of key events in the history of Portmagee and its development is included as Appendix C for quick reference. 

You may want to check that the information you are providing is being absorbed by your audience. So a few questions along the way can be included in your script and it is a good way to get an audience to interact. Your tour content should help people answer the following questions.

1. Who is Portmagee named after?
2. Why is Portmagee Whiskey finished in Barbados Rum Casks?
3. What is a Seine Boat?
4. What happens at the Portmagee Regatta?
5. What is the connection with the Murphy family to Portmagee?
6. What is the significance of the RIC Barracks to the Portmagee story?
7. What are the future plans of Portmagee Whiskey?

Delivering your tour with confidence

There are a number of ways that you can practice and perfect your tour:
• Practice on friends, colleagues – and family!
• Attend other guided tours in area and further afield
• Read around tour subjects and develop your own favourite subject area
• Have fun and enjoy it. 



Boosting Business

As a Portmagee tour guide you are central to the visitor experience. You can also play a role in helping the business to become sustainable. Here are a few.
* Promote the facilities and services at Portmagee to encourage longer stays, additional expenditure and repeat business. For example you can point out the café, shop and toilet facilities and suggest guests spend time exploring the site independently, you can remind guests to recommend Portmagee Whiskey to their friends and family and say that you hope to see them again soon.
* Upselling, for example at Portmagee people can buy a bottle of Whiskey that they have sampled. If you can identify the decision maker in a couple, family or group and engage with them.
* Use your customer service skills to boost business, for example if someone enquires about a future tour or a special event at Portmagee make sure you direct them to a staff member who can respond to their interest.
* Work with your crewmembers as an effective guiding team. Ensure that there are opportunities within your tour for people to spend money on refreshments and gifts. Wherever possible end the tour at a place where they can purchase add ons and ensure your co-workers are expecting them, for example inform café staff the approximate time that your tour finishes so they can anticipate an influx of customers.

Sustainability Points to Note:

* Portmagee Whiskey is a Bord Bia – Origin Green certified company since 2018 and are currently into our second 5-year sustainability plan.
* Portmagee Whiskey is a certified member of the Sustainable Tourism Network certification scheme.
* Our native Irish Woodlands has 5,430 trees planted including 550 oak trees and we will be developing a nature trail in this woodlands over the coming years. Other trees planted include Hazel, Rowan, Birch, Scots Pine and Alder. Certain sections of the fields have been left blank, unmown, un-grazed to allow native wild flowers to bloom and are always changing colour wit the seasons. Lots of native wildlife has returned to the area including birds, bats, rabbits, hares and hedgehogs which were not as prevalent when the land was farmed for cattle by our grandparents.
* The Portmagee Channel and Valentia Harbour are a special area of conservation so extra measures are in place to protect the harbour and marine life present.
* The Skelligs Islands has a protected bird sanctuary on the Little Skelligs and both islands and the surrounding waters are part of Irelands first National Marine Park that stretches up to Dingle peninsula to our North.



Cultural Points to Note:

* Be sure to mention the Intangible Cultural Heritage status of Seine Boat Building, Fishing and Racing and the significance and role that Portmagee Whiskey had to play in achieving that status in 2021.
* The Skellig Islands landing tours are a great privilege and opportunity to step into another world and time. That privilege comes with responsibilities and at the start of the tour, a Skellig tour guide who will be a qualified historian or park ranger will set out guidelines such as leave no trace, don’t disturb the native flora and fauna, stay on the paths and don’t take home rocks as souvenirs etc. as well as general safety tips for the 618 steps to be climbed to reach the monastery.
* Drinking responsibly is to everyone’s benefit, whiskey is to be enjoyed and not endured and it must be conscious that it is a very strong spirit in terms of ABV. Make sure visitors don’t drink too much and take water when needed also. A nice cultural experience is the goal here.
* Some locations around South Kerry, including the RIC Barracks have deep connections to both Ireland’s War of Independence and Civil War. Due respect should be afforded these difficult elements of local history. Visitors from foreign countries who are confused or unaware of differences between IRA during the War of Independence to the current paramilitary gangs using the name should be educated on this important difference. When explaining issues such as famine times or economic, social struggles of Ireland under English and British rule, be cognisant of how the stories are told and entire peoples should not be belittled or made to feel to blame for the actions of previous generations etc.  





Annex A: Display Board Information

(1.1) Welcome
With every bottle of Portmagee Whiskey, we aim to tell the story of our village. From entrepreneurial smugglers, to hardy fishermen rowing Seine Boats and battling the Wild Atlantic Ocean. Please enjoy discovering the wonderful tales and tribulations from the village of Portmagee, our home beside the sea.

(2.1) A Village With A Notorious Name
The influence of Theobald Magee
The village of ‘Portmagee’ is named after Captain Theobald Magee - a 17th-century Army Officer who served under King James II and fought at the Battle of the Boyne in 1690. 
Defeated, he turned to a life of merchant shipping between France, Portugal, Spain and here, in Ireland. 
Thanks to the many inlets around the South West coast of Ireland, his trade in contraband spirits, textiles, tea and tobacco was hard to police and a profitable enterprise. 
The local fishing village was at the heart of Theobald’s operations - which made him both synonymous in the region and revered locally for his cunning. 
Forget fiction, Captain Magee was a very real smuggler of the high seas.

(3.1) Captain Magee
The ultimate lawbreaker
Despite his local prominence, little is known about Captain Theobald Magee, (1666 to 1727) - after all, he did make most of his fortune through illegal trading of sought after goods. 
With his wealth, he bought large amounts of property across Cork and Kerry and married Bridget Morgell, the widower of a rich merchant from Dingle. 
Together they created a family and a powerful trade partnership. It was this partnership, however, that may have been the death of the Captain. 
One local rumour suggests that Captain Magee was exiled abroad by Bridget’s father - a powerful man who was a Member of Parliament for County Kerry and ashamed of the family connection to the smuggler. It is said that the Captain went to a Portuguese monastery for three years, where he died in 1727 by poisoning - never to return to Ireland. 
In his will, he gave farmland to his three sons and the smuggling business continued with Bridget at the helm. Even after his death, Captain Magee was still the ultimate lawbreaker.

(4.1) The Fishing Village Of Portmagee
A local industry for generations
The village has built itself upon a long relationship with the fishing industry, which continues to this day. In 1825, as many as 271 boats were recorded as fishing in the local area, with Pilchards and Mackerel the most in-demand species. 
Now, Portmagee remains home to a small number of locally-owned trawlers that specialise in catching fresh fish from the Wild Atlantic waters. Fresh lobsters and crabs caught in Portmagee’s clean waters are sold around the world. 
Fishing, although not as universally done as it once was, remains at the very heart of our community and village. 
Our fishermen take pride in their fishing heritage and continue to face the wilds of the Atlantic on many a cold, bitter and dark night - guided not only by modern technologies but also by wisdom passed down through generations. 



(5.1) Introduction Of Seine Boats
Reinventing the local economy
As the smuggling trade began to dry up and British rule extended throughout the Irish countryside, it was the introduction of Seine Boats that reinvigorated the local economy. With these new fishing vessels and methods, locals were encouraged to fish the abundance of species in the Wild Atlantic Ocean. 
From spring through autumn, hundreds of Seine Boats (each attended by a smaller boat – the follower) operated out of Portmagee and many other small coves and harbours. The Seine Boats ranged in size from 25ft to 34ft, with a maximum beam of 7 feet. Propelled by up to twelve oarsmen on double-banked oars, they carried a helmsman or ‘Captain’ and sometimes a ‘Hewer’ or fish spotter in the bow. The smaller follower carried a maximum of six oarsmen and a helmsman.

Men would fish at night, using the moonlight to spot shoals of fish glistening in the sea, commonly referred to as the ‘Barr t-Iasc’. The follower would hold a position as the Seine Boat encircled the shoal with the Seine net and as the boats came together again, great effort was exerted to haul the net up with as many fish caught as possible.

Once returned to Portmagee, the women of the village, including the wives of the fishermen, would salt and pack the fish in wooden casks ready for transportation to the rest of Ireland and mainland Britain. The introduction of the railway to South Kerry in the late 19th century meant Portmagee had a quicker connection to the main market of London. Fish were sold the very next day in England’s capital and became an increasingly lucrative destination for the catch.

(6.1) From Fishing Boats To Racing Boats
The heritage of the Seine Boat continues
The 1950s saw the modernisation of fishing practices with the introduction of two motorised fishing boats, ‘Puma’ and ‘Lynx’. 
The Lynx was partly owned by John ‘Barrack’ Murphy the 1st, our Great-grandfather. 
This mechanisation brought about the decline of the Seine Boat as a fishing method. However, in 1955, during the Portmagee Regatta, a high-profile Seine Boat race would change the fate of the Seine Boat, forever. 
The local O’Mahoney crew of Ardcost challenged the rest of Ireland to a Seine Boat race with Ł100 on offer for the winner - €3,000 in today’s money. 
Despite losing to the only crew who accepted the challenge, from nearby Ballanskelligs, the race helped rejuvenate the Seine Boats’ fortunes. 
The traditions of racing and making boats in the region continues to this day. Due to its sheltered harbour, Portmagee is viewed as the favourite place for Seine Boat regattas and hosts the single most important Seine Boat race of the calendar year. 

(7.1) Johnny Mahoney
The greatest ever Seine Boatbuilder
The name of the greatest ever Seine Boatbuilder is immortalised on the prize for the regions’ Seine Boat Crew Of The Year - The Johnny Mahoney Cup.

This hardy former fisherman was also a hugely successful oarsman in his younger years, racing together with his brothers, whose feats in the sea made them local legends. Johnny used those decades of experience on the waves to breathe new life into the sport of Seine Boat racing - and bring with it a very modern approach. 
Alongside constructing the fastest boats, he also became the coach of many young crews and helped steer them to victory. He encouraged the good ones, cajoled those whose dedication left something to be desired and was always there to give last-minute encouragement and advice on the day of the regatta. 
He was unique, he was incomparable and he was hugely successful. There was only one Johnny O’Mahoney and he left his mark through the incredible craftsmanship on every boat he built. 

(8.1) The Seine Boat Master Builders
Honouring generations of craftsmen
Many families throughout the last three centuries have been involved in building and repairing Seine Boats for local fishermen and racing crews. 
Many of these families have passed down this traditional skill, from father to son, crafting the boats by eye. 
Now with a declining population and Seine Boats being used only for racing, fewer people know how to build these wonderful boats. 
Since the 1970s, Seine Boat designs have been standardised in the region, although clubs continue to customise their boats for greater speed. 
All boats are made from a combination of wood, including oak and elm, and can only be a maximum of 34 feet long (10.3 metres). The widest part of the boat from gunnel to gunnel must be no wider than 6 foot 3 inches (1.905m) wide. 
Johnny O’Mahoney was the most famous boatbuilder of them all. A Portmagee man who built many of the finest and most successful racing Seine Boats over the decades.

(9.1) Smuggler’s Paradise
Risking life for great riches
Exile to labour camps in Australia and the death penalty were the harshest punishments given out to leaders of smuggling gangs. 
Despite the severity of the punishment, Portmagee became an epi-centre of the contraband trade in Ireland. 
The notorious Captain Magee was one of the first to spot the potential of the port, partly because it was so far away from the Crown authorities, but also because locals would have been willing to keep their dealings quiet. 
Teas, tobacco, textiles and spirits were shipped from the New Americas, as well as Catholic countries such as France, Spain and Portugal. 
From Portmagee, these goods were divided and sent to merchants around Ireland, and ironically to the centre of the British Empire, where the ruling classes would buy them for high prices. 
Among the spirits smuggled into Ireland would have been Barbados Rum. Today in homage to our villages smuggling traditions, we finish all our whiskey in Barbados Rum casks. 

(10.1) Monks & Vikings
The holy retreat plundered by Vikings
8 miles (13km) out to sea is the famous Skellig Islands, a sacred retreat for both Pagans and Christians. For the Irish monks in the 6th Century, Skellig Michael became a monastic settlement and was described as the “edge of the world” by monks making the perilous journey from Portmagee Harbour. 
For all its natural beauty and religious importance, Skellig Michael was also vulnerable to attack. The Vikings raided the island on a number of occasions, before making their way towards Portmagee and pillaging the nearby island of Illaunloghan (pronounced: ee-lawn-lock-awn). The violence of these raids and sheer terror inflicted on the peaceful monks shaped the future of Ireland. Several conflicts with Vikings occurred until eventually, the most famous battle took place, led by the Irish High King Brian Boru and his victory at the Battle of Clontarf in 1014.

(Smugglers walk 1.1)
Captain Magee’s Crew - Would you resist the fortunes?
Captain Magee was more than just a successful merchant in the eyes of locals, he was also respected for his position as an Army Officer under King James II. 
He brought jobs, trade, sought-after goods from the continent and prosperity to an impoverished region. For his sailors, they had the chance to sail to far off lands such as France, Portugal and Spain - returning as well paid heroes having had the experience of a lifetime abroad. 
Villagers would be part of the onshore team to help disperse the trade, hide it from the Red Coats and smuggle it across the country to wealthy customers - taking a cut of the profits along the way. 
Would you turn down the chance to make more money? Or, would you steer clear of the smuggling happening around you?

(Smugglers walk 2.1)
The Romance Is Fading - Are you still willing to smuggle?
Captain Magee’s marriage to Bridget Morgell left a lot for locals to ponder. Bridget's Father, Thomas Crosby, held a position as a Member of Parliament for County Kerry and had strong business connections with the Port of Dingle. The illegal activities of the smuggling trade in Portmagee in which his daughter was implicated, had the potential for political embarrassment at the time.
Despite Theobald and Bridget having a family, the romance was always at odds with the expectations of the ruling class. The romance turned to tragedy when Captain Magee was exiled to Portugal, never to return to Ireland. 
It is rumoured he was poisoned to death in Lisbon. Bridget didn’t spend long mourning her dead husband though, as the smuggling trade continued to flourish. Perhaps the illegal trading was made more palatable for locals, knowing it was in honour of the much-admired Captain Magee. Now he is dead, would you still be willing to smuggle for Bridget’s empire?  

(Smugglers walk 3.1)
The Authorities Arrive - What is your next move?
The British rule had caught onto the secrets of Portmagee. The very building in front of you was the former Barracks of the Royal Irish Constabulary (RIC), built to send a message of law and order to the smuggler’s paradise along the coast. Here is where the law was enforced and in time illegal trading was stamped out. With the authorities within walking distance of your home, would you continue to smuggle?

Without this building, Portmagee Whiskey would not exist. For it was here that a young RIC policeman, our Great-great-grandfather Sylvester Murphy, was posted to serve. It is here that six generations of the Murphy family have called home. 
This building brought law and order to a smuggler's paradise and survived the Fenian uprisings. It was considered an IRA safe house during the Irish War of Independence only to be attacked and burned during the 1922-1923 Irish Civil War. 
It was then turned into a family home before being torn down for lack of money to pay property tax. It was also reused as a cowshed before falling into disrepair. It is now the centre of the Portmagee Whiskey adventure. 

Once again we will see fine spirits shipped from Portmagee to the far reaches of the world, starting their journey from this historic place, a piece of our village’s intriguing history. 

We are proud to welcome you to Portmagee, Sláinte.

(Viewer Info board)
Through the looking glass
The fields in front of you were key to the livelihoods of generations of Portmagee families. It was here that many of the fish curing houses or ‘Pallaces’ were built. The women of the village played a key role as they took the lead in splitting, gutting and salting the fish before packing them into wooden casks for transportation and onward sale. 

The work was gruelling, often taking place at unforgiving hours. Once the fisherman in their Seine Boats returned to Portmagee harbour and the pier, the fish had to be processed immediately regardless of weather or time of day. Children too were regularly involved and it was not uncommon for entire families, father, mother and children to be fully involved in all aspects of the fishing industry.

Look through the viewer and try to imagine how Portmagee harbour looked from this hill in centuries gone by. From Captain Magee’s smuggling ships, Seine Boat fishermen landing their catch and the hard-working Portmagee women ashore.
