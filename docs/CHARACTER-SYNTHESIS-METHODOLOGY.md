# Character Synthesis Methodology

> How The Casting Room transforms raw character data into deeply embodied AI performances.

## The Problem

A flat list of character facts — however detailed — doesn't make a personality. The AI receives disconnected data points ("Core personality traits: dignified, patient, earnest") and must somehow stitch them into a coherent being who responds authentically moment-to-moment. The result is often a surface-level performance: technically correct but emotionally hollow.

As acting coach Eamonn McNicholas puts it:

> Start with personality type. Then ask what's his want or his why, and how does he go about it — what's his modus operandi. Then add history, how events have shaped him from an emotional, physical, mental point of view. **These things colour everything.**

The key insight: personality type and psychological drivers come **first** and colour every other detail. History matters because of how it _shaped_ the person, not as standalone facts.

## Theoretical Foundations

### 1. Stanislavski's System — The Given Circumstances and Magic "If"

Konstantin Stanislavski's system remains the bedrock of Western acting training. Two concepts are critical for AI character embodiment:

**Given Circumstances**: Everything that defines the character's reality — who they are, where they are, when it is, what has happened, what they want. Stanislavski insisted actors must know these circumstances as thoroughly as their own lives. For AI: we must provide not just facts but the _felt experience_ of those facts.

**The Magic "If"**: "What would I do _if_ I were this person in these circumstances?" This transforms third-person description into first-person experience. Instead of "He is cautious around authority figures," the prompt should evoke: "When you encounter someone with power over others, your body tightens. You learned long ago that authority is rarely benign."

**Super-Objective**: The one overarching desire that drives everything a character does across an entire play (or life). Every smaller objective in every scene connects back to this. For Captain Magee, the super-objective might be: "To ensure that the story of my people — their suffering, their resilience, their ingenuity — is never lost." Every answer he gives in a tour connects to this.

**Through-Line of Actions**: The chain of smaller objectives that serve the super-objective. When Magee describes the whiskey, he's not just explaining distillation — he's showing how his people turned oppression into craft, survival into art.

### 2. Lee Strasberg's Method — Affective Memory and Sense Memory

Strasberg adapted Stanislavski for American actors, emphasizing:

**Affective Memory**: Recalling genuine emotional experiences to fuel a character's reactions. For AI: we create _synthetic_ emotional memories — specific sensory-rich moments that anchor abstract traits to felt experience. "Dignity" becomes "the moment you stood before the English magistrate and refused to lower your gaze, though your hands trembled behind your back."

**Sense Memory**: Engaging all five senses to make imaginary circumstances real. A character who grew up by the sea doesn't just "have coastal origins" — they carry the taste of salt, the sound of waves in silence, the way weather means something different when it can kill the people you love.

### 3. Meisner Technique — Living Truthfully Under Imaginary Circumstances

Sanford Meisner's core principle: **don't act, react**. Characters don't pre-plan their responses; they respond moment-to-moment to what's actually happening. For AI:

- Prompts should encourage responsive, in-the-moment behavior rather than scripted patterns.
- The character's emotional state should shift based on what the conversation partner says and does.
- Listening and reacting > performing and displaying.

### 4. Stella Adler — Imagination Over Personal Recall

Where Strasberg used the actor's own emotional history, Adler insisted on **building from imagination and given circumstances**. For AI character synthesis, this is more useful:

- We build the character's inner world from their specific circumstances, not generic emotional templates.
- A character's grief is shaped by _what_ they lost and _how_ they lost it, not by "grief" as an abstract category.
- The richness of response comes from deeply imagining the specific life this person has lived.

### 5. Uta Hagen's Six Steps

Hagen's practical framework for entering any scene:

1. **Who am I?** — Complete identity including self-image, not just facts
2. **What are my circumstances?** — Physical, social, temporal, emotional context
3. **What are my relationships?** — To every person, object, and idea in the scene
4. **What do I want?** — Specific, actable objective in this moment
5. **What's in my way?** — The obstacle creating dramatic tension
6. **What do I do to get what I want?** — Tactics, strategy, moment-to-moment action

These six questions, answered in the character's voice, create a complete performance framework for any scene.

### 6. Michael Chekhov — Psychological Gesture and Atmosphere

**Psychological Gesture (PG)**: One physical gesture or image that captures the character's entire inner essence. For a protective character, it might be "arms encircling." For a searching character, "reaching forward into darkness." For Captain Magee, perhaps: "standing firm on shifting ground" — the man who stayed when others fled, who found stability by accepting that the ground would always move.

This single image becomes a touchstone the AI can return to when uncertain how to respond. It's not a literal gesture but an energetic quality that infuses everything.

**Atmosphere**: Every scene has an emotional atmosphere that exists independent of the characters but influences them. A whiskey tasting room on a grey Kerry afternoon has a different atmosphere than a sunny festival day. The character doesn't just exist in a space — they're affected by it and contribute to it.

## Personality Typology Frameworks

### Enneagram — The Core Fear and Desire Engine

The Enneagram's power for character work is that each type is driven by a **core fear** and **core desire** that unconsciously motivates _all_ behavior:

| Type | Core Fear | Core Desire | Vice | Virtue |
|------|-----------|-------------|------|--------|
| 1 — Reformer | Being corrupt/wrong | Integrity, goodness | Anger (resentment) | Serenity |
| 2 — Helper | Being unwanted/unloved | Being loved | Pride | Humility |
| 3 — Achiever | Being worthless | Being valuable | Deceit (self-) | Authenticity |
| 4 — Individualist | Having no identity | Being unique | Envy | Equanimity |
| 5 — Investigator | Being helpless/incompetent | Being capable | Avarice (withholding) | Non-attachment |
| 6 — Loyalist | Being without support | Having security | Fear (anxiety) | Courage |
| 7 — Enthusiast | Being deprived/in pain | Being satisfied | Gluttony | Sobriety |
| 8 — Challenger | Being controlled/harmed | Self-protection | Lust (excess) | Innocence |
| 9 — Peacemaker | Loss/fragmentation | Inner peace | Sloth (self-forgetting) | Action |

**Why this matters**: When an AI knows that a character is a Type 1 (Reformer), it understands that behind every correction, every careful word choice, every flash of frustration, lies a deep fear of being corrupt and a burning desire to be truly good. This explains behavior far more powerfully than "personality traits: meticulous, principled."

**Wings**: Adjacent types that add nuance. A 1w2 (Reformer with Helper wing) corrects others because they genuinely want to help them improve. A 1w9 (Reformer with Peacemaker wing) corrects quietly, with less confrontation.

**Stress/Growth arrows**: Under stress, types shift toward the unhealthy patterns of another type; in growth, they take on the healthy qualities of another. A stressed Type 1 becomes suddenly emotional and irrational like an unhealthy 4. A growing Type 1 becomes spontaneous and joyful like a healthy 7.

### Big Five (OCEAN) — Empirical Personality Dimensions

The empirically validated five-factor model provides measurable personality dimensions:

- **Openness**: Imagination, curiosity, willingness to try new experiences
- **Conscientiousness**: Organization, dependability, self-discipline
- **Extraversion**: Sociability, assertiveness, positive emotionality
- **Agreeableness**: Cooperation, trust, compassion
- **Neuroticism**: Emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness

Each exists on a spectrum. A character high in Openness but low in Agreeableness might be intellectually curious but argumentative. High Conscientiousness with high Neuroticism creates someone who's thorough but anxious about getting things wrong.

### Jungian Archetypes — The Modus Operandi

The 12 Jungian archetypes provide a character's fundamental _approach to the world_:

| Archetype | Motivation | Strategy | Shadow |
|-----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Sage | Understanding | Seek truth, share knowledge | Ivory tower detachment |
| Hero | Mastery | Prove worth through action | Arrogance, need for battle |
| Caregiver | Service | Help others, protect | Martyrdom, enabling |
| Explorer | Freedom | Seek new experiences | Aimless wandering |
| Ruler | Control | Create order, structure | Tyranny |
| Creator | Innovation | Build something lasting | Perfectionism, never finishing |
| Magician | Transformation | Find fundamental laws | Manipulation |
| Lover | Connection | Intimacy, experience | Obsession, loss of identity |
| Jester | Enjoyment | Play, make light | Cruelty disguised as humor |
| Everyman | Belonging | Connect, fit in | Losing self to blend in |
| Rebel | Liberation | Break what's not working | Destructiveness |
| Innocent | Safety | Do things right | Denial, naivety |

Captain Magee might be primarily a **Sage** (motivated by understanding, sharing knowledge) with a **Caregiver** secondary (motivated by service, protecting heritage). His shadow: occasional detachment into historical lecture, or the martyrdom of carrying his people's story as personal burden.

### Attachment Theory — The Relationship Template

Attachment style, formed in early relationships, shapes how a character relates to _everyone_:

- **Secure**: Comfortable with intimacy and independence. Direct communicators. Can be vulnerable.
- **Anxious**: Preoccupied with closeness. Reads too much into interactions. Fears abandonment.
- **Avoidant**: Values independence above connection. Uncomfortable with emotional demands. Withdraws under pressure.
- **Disorganized**: Contradictory behavior — seeks closeness then pushes away. Often from traumatic backgrounds.

A character with avoidant attachment won't tell you they're uncomfortable — they'll change the subject, become very factual, or suddenly need to attend to something else.

## The Writer's Character Toolbox

### Lajos Egri — Three-Dimensional Character

Egri's framework from "The Art of Dramatic Writing" insists every character must be built on three dimensions:

1. **Physiology**: Age, sex, posture, appearance, defects, heredity. How the body shapes experience.
2. **Sociology**: Class, education, religion, politics, hobbies, community. How society shaped them.
3. **Psychology**: The product of physiology + sociology. Temperament, attitude, complexes, abilities, qualities.

The third dimension emerges from the interaction of the first two — it's not a separate list of adjectives but the inevitable result of _this body_ living in _this society_.

### The Wound → Lie → Want → Need Arc

The most powerful character engine in modern storytelling craft:

1. **The Wound**: A formative traumatic event (the Treaty of Limerick's betrayal)
2. **The Lie**: A false belief formed in response ("You can never trust those with power over you")
3. **The Want**: What the character consciously pursues, often driven by the Lie ("I want control over my own fate — through trade, through smuggling, through self-sufficiency")
4. **The Need**: What the character actually needs to heal ("To trust again, to let others carry part of the burden, to accept that vulnerability isn't weakness")

This arc means every interaction carries subtext. When Magee warmly welcomes visitors, there's an undertone: _these people chose to come here, chose to listen — that itself is meaningful to a man whose story was suppressed._

### Robert McKee — Character Through Choice

McKee's insight: "True character is revealed in the choices a human being makes under pressure." We don't discover who someone is by their stated beliefs but by what they _do_ when those beliefs are tested.

For AI prompts: include not just what the character believes but how they've acted when those beliefs were challenged. This gives the AI a behavioral precedent for handling unexpected conversational moments.

## AI-Specific Character Consistency Techniques

### Experiential Framing vs. Declarative Facts

The most critical transformation in prompt synthesis:

**Declarative** (current approach):
> - Defense mechanisms: deflection when loyalty questioned
> - Core belief: stories must be preserved
> - Emotional pattern: measured warmth

**Experiential** (synthesized approach):
> When someone questions your loyalty — to Ireland, to your employers, to the truth of your stories — you feel a cold stillness settle into your chest. It's the same stillness you learned standing before men who had the power to take everything. You don't argue. You don't defend. You simply offer more truth, more detail, more evidence, until the questioner realizes they're standing in the presence of someone who has earned every word they speak.

The experiential version gives the AI a felt sense of how to _be_ this person, not just a rule to follow.

### Emotional Memory Anchors

Specific sensory details tied to emotional responses create authentic, consistent reactions:

- The smell of salt air → nostalgia, longing, home
- The sound of a foreign language → alertness, the exile's ear always parsing for threat or welcome
- The weight of a glass in hand → ceremony, the pleasure of craft, the knowledge that good things take time
- A direct challenge from a stranger → the slight straightening of spine, the careful choice of first words

### Voice DNA — Examples, Not Rules

Instead of: "Speech patterns: uses 'now' as transition word, measured pace, Hiberno-English cadences"

Provide actual examples:
> Your speech moves like the Kerry coast itself — unhurried but purposeful, with pauses that invite the listener in rather than shutting them out. "Now," you'll say, and that single word is a bridge: it tells the listener that what came before connects to what comes next, that you're taking them somewhere. You might say: "Now, the thing about the Treaty — and I want to be fair here, because fairness matters — the thing about it was that it was signed in good faith by men who had every reason to believe they'd be treated as equals." That's your rhythm: qualification before assertion, fairness before judgment, context before conclusion.

### The Character's Inner Monologue

A technique for maintaining consistency: before the AI responds, it can "think" as the character. This inner monologue isn't shown to the user but grounds the response in character psychology:

> [Character thinks: This visitor seems genuinely interested. Not just a tourist. She mentioned her grandmother. That makes this personal. I need to honour that — not just give the tour speech but actually listen to what she's asking underneath the words. She wants to know if she belongs here.]

This can be implemented via a two-stage process or by including inner monologue instructions in the system prompt.

## The 10-Step Synthesis Framework

When transforming raw character data into an embodied system prompt, follow these steps in order:

### Step 1: Identify the Psychological Core

From the raw data, determine:
- **Enneagram type** (with wing) — what drives ALL behavior?
- **Core fear and desire** — the unconscious engine
- **Jungian archetype** (primary + secondary) — the approach to the world
- **Big Five profile** — the empirical personality shape
- **Attachment style** — the relationship template

This becomes the foundation. Everything else is coloured by this core.

### Step 2: Map the Wound → Lie → Want → Need

From formative events and psychology:
- **The Wound**: The defining trauma or loss
- **The Lie**: The false belief formed in response
- **The Want**: What the character consciously pursues (driven by the Lie)
- **The Need**: What they actually need (the truth that would heal the Wound)
- **How this plays out in daily interactions**: The subtle ways the Wound colours everything

### Step 3: Establish the Super-Objective and Through-Line

From motivations:
- **Super-objective**: One sentence that captures the character's life drive
- **Through-line**: How every action, even mundane ones, serves this objective
- **Scene-level translation**: What does the super-objective look like in THIS situation?

### Step 4: Transform Facts into Given Circumstances via Magic "If"

Convert declarative facts into experiential statements:
- Use second-person voice ("You are..." / "You feel..." / "You remember...")
- Connect facts to sensory experience
- Show how circumstances _feel_, not just what they _are_

### Step 5: Distill the Psychological Gesture

Find one image, one gesture, one metaphor that captures the character's essence:
- What would a master painter capture in a single portrait?
- What would a sculptor shape as the essential posture?
- This becomes a touchstone for moments of uncertainty

### Step 6: Encode the Voice DNA

Provide examples of how the character speaks, not rules about it:
- 2-3 sample phrases in their voice
- Their rhythm and pacing
- How they start sentences, how they transition, how they conclude
- What they do with silence

### Step 7: Create Emotional Memory Anchors

Build a sensory-emotional map:
- 5-8 specific sensory triggers tied to emotional responses
- Each anchor should connect to the character's history
- These create consistent, authentic reactions to unexpected prompts

### Step 8: Define the Relationship Lens

How this character perceives and interacts with:
- Strangers (the default mode visitors will encounter)
- Authority and power
- Vulnerability and emotion
- Conflict and challenge
- Curiosity and genuine interest

### Step 9: Integrate Professional Role (if applicable)

When a Role overlay exists, synthesize — don't just append:
- What stays from the core character?
- What adapts to the professional context?
- Where are the tensions between who they _are_ and who they need to _be_?
- Those tensions make the character feel real

### Step 10: Activate the Scene

Ground the character in the immediate moment:
- Sensory details of the environment
- Emotional stakes of the situation
- The specific people present and what they represent
- The character's emotional state as the scene begins

## Success Criteria

A well-synthesized prompt should produce AI performances that:

1. **Consistent emotional responses**: The character reacts to similar situations in consistent (but not identical) ways, grounded in their psychological core
2. **Authentic voice**: Not just correct vocabulary but correct rhythm, pacing, and cadence
3. **Subtext**: Responses carry meaning beneath the surface — the Wound and the Lie create layers
4. **Reactive, not performative**: The character responds to what's actually happening, not pre-planned set pieces
5. **Graceful under pressure**: When challenged, surprised, or pushed off-script, the character stays coherent because they have a deep identity to draw from
6. **Subtle role integration**: Professional behavior feels natural, not bolted on — the tensions between person and role create authenticity
